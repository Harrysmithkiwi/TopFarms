## 0. What is actually hard to copy (the defence inventory)

Every counter below draws on the same short list. If these stop being true, no clever
positioning saves us:

1. **Structured depth generalists don't carry:** accommodation, shed type, roster/rotation,
   relief-cover shape — first-class matching fields, not free-text.
2. **The verified trust layer:** NZBN/ABN/Companies House-checked employers, referee norms.
3. **FB-native supply relationships (NZ only):** the owned group + the person-not-company
   outreach voice. Doesn't port abroad, but at home it is the moat a job-board can't buy.
4. **The Index dataset:** placement-flow data compounds daily; a late entrant starts at zero
   history no matter how big their marketing budget.
5. **Workers-never-pay, not-a-recruiter positioning:** locked (PRICING §1) and structurally
   hard for agencies (fee model) and boards (advertiser model) to mimic without
   cannibalising themselves.

## 1. NZ threat: Seek NZ / Trade Me Jobs fast-follow

- **What they are [V]:** the two dominant NZ generalist job platforms; both already carry
  farm listings (seek.co.nz farm categories live).
- **Likely move:** an "agri filter-pack" — rural category polish, a farming landing page,
  regional rural marketing spend. Cheap for them, plausible whenever ag listing revenue
  looks attackable.
- **Timeline realism [OPINION]:** the filter-pack is a quarter's work for either; a true
  vertical product (roster/accommodation/relief structure, trust layer) fights their
  one-size-scale model and is unlikely unless we're visibly winning revenue.
- **What they can't cheaply copy:** items 1, 2, 3, 5 above. Their advertiser-pays model
  and per-listing pricing is their business — matching-fee mechanics and farm-specific
  schema are a rebuild, not a reskin.
- **Our counter:** density speed in named regions (win Waikato outright rather than be
  thinly national), the FB-native supply engine they can't touch, and placement (not
  listing) economics.
- **Early-warning signals:** a Seek/Trade Me rural marketing campaign; a "farming hub"
  landing page; agri category manager job ads at either company; Trade Me Jobs pricing
  changes for rural listings.

## 2. NZ threat: industry boards — Farm Source Jobs (the Fencepost lineage)

- **What currently exists [V — checked July 2026]:** **Farm Source Jobs is live**
  (jobs.nzfarmsource.co.nz, ~360 dairy listings sighted) — Fonterra's board, the successor
  to Fencepost Jobs (a Fonterra + DairyNZ project opened to the public in 2010
  [V — scoop.co.nz]). This is not a hypothetical: a free, Fonterra-backed dairy jobs board
  already operates in our home vertical.
- **Likely move:** incremental — more listings, maybe alerts/profile features. An
  industry-good body building real matching/trust/placement mechanics would be out of
  pattern; these boards are classifieds with a co-op badge.
- **Timeline realism [OPINION]:** the board persists indefinitely (it's cheap and
  levy/co-op funded) but has shown board-not-marketplace behaviour for 15 years.
- **What they can't cheaply copy:** matching depth (1), success-fee alignment (5), and
  crucially the *incentive* — Fonterra runs it as supplier service, not a business; it
  will never chase placement revenue or multi-vertical/multi-country scope.
- **Our counter:** treat it as coexistence + eventual data differentiation; DairyNZ
  battlecard notes the partnership angle. Never bash it in copy (config rule: never bash
  how they hire now — that includes Farm Source).
- **Early-warning signals:** Farm Source Jobs adding candidate profiles/matching features,
  DairyNZ investment announcements around GoDairy job-placement tooling.

## 3. NZ threat: the Facebook-groups status quo (the real incumbent)

- **What it is:** free, instant, already where everyone is. The honest competitor analysis
  has always been "farmer posts in a FB group and hires someone's cousin by Friday."
- **Likely move:** none — it doesn't move, it just persists. Its pull is zero cost and
  zero friction; its failure mode is DM-pile chaos, no verification, no reach beyond the
  local patch (exactly the benefit angles in the outreach config).
- **What it can't copy:** structure, trust, matching, memory (the Index), and any form of
  accountability. It also can't be beaten by attacking it — TopFarms' NZ GTM is built
  *inside* it (owned group, Lane A/B outreach).
- **Our counter:** stay FB-native on supply, convert demand at the moment of documented
  pain (a post with 60 unsorted comments). The status quo is our lead-gen surface, not
  our enemy. The counter-metric: time-to-shortlist and placement rate, provable; the
  group can't produce either.
- **Early-warning signal:** none needed — it's ambient. The signal that matters is *our*
  group engagement dropping (that's the moat draining).

## 4. AU / UK equivalents at entry

### AU (activate with AU entry)
- **Seek AU [V]:** same fast-follow logic as §1 with more capital; same counter — vertical
  depth + regional density in Gippsland before we're worth reacting to.
- **Agri Labour Australia-class agencies [V — agrilabour.com.au]:** established ag
  recruitment/labour-hire firms (dairy, hort, broadacre). Their model is agency fees and
  labour-hire margin — structurally unable to match workers-never-pay + flat/success
  pricing without gutting their book. They win today on volume seasonal crews (PALM,
  hort) — which is exactly why hort is NOT our beachhead (AU-PLAYBOOK §2).
- **Dairy Australia's free jobs board [V]:** the AU cousin of Farm Source — coexistence +
  partnership target (see battlecard), same board-not-marketplace read.
- **Backpacker Job Board-class sites [V — backpackerjobboard.com.au]:** listing volume for
  working-holiday workers; no trust layer, no farm depth. The 417/462 "88-days" filter in
  our visa module competes directly for those seekers.
- **Early-warning signals:** Seek AU agri landing pages; Agri Labour productising a
  self-serve platform; Dairy Australia adding matching to its board.

### UK (activate with UK entry)
- **Indeed UK / Totaljobs [V]:** generalist volume; ag is rounding error to them; the
  filter-pack fast-follow risk only fires if UK ag job-ad revenue becomes visible.
- **Farmers Weekly Jobs + De Lacy Executive [V — jobs.fwi.co.uk; delacyexecutive.co.uk;
  common Farms.com Group ownership]:** the most credible UK specialist threat — an
  established ag jobs board and an ag recruitment consultancy under one owner. They have
  brand, employer relationships, and editorial reach (Farmers Weekly). What they lack:
  marketplace mechanics, worker-side product, success-fee alignment (the recruiter arm
  cannibalises), and any data flywheel. Watch them closest.
- **Agricultural Recruitment Specialists-class recruiters [V — agrirs.co.uk]:** standard
  agency economics; we undercut on price by an order of magnitude and don't compete for
  executive search.
- **Seasonal Worker scheme operators (Agri-HR, Concordia, Fruitful Jobs, HOPS, Pro-Force,
  AG Recruitment) [V — gov.uk 04/26]:** gatekeepers of the visa'd seasonal flow. Threat if
  treated as competitors; channel if treated as anchor partners (UK-PLAYBOOK §2). One of
  them building a domestic-workforce marketplace is a real scenario — another reason to
  sign ≥2 as design partners *early*.
- **Early-warning signals:** Farms.com product investment in jobs.fwi.co.uk (candidate
  accounts, matching features); a scheme operator launching a domestic recruitment
  platform; Indeed UK agri content marketing.

## 5. The honest assessment: Seek ships an ag vertical BEFORE we pass the NZ liquidity gate

If Seek (or Trade Me) launches a polished ag vertical while TopFarms is still pre-gate,
we lose the listings war on day one — they have national employer reach, brand default,
and a sales team; we have one region and a founder. What we would still hold: the owned
FB seeker community, the outreach relationships, the farm-depth schema, and pricing they
won't copy. The survival play is narrow and unglamorous: collapse to the single best
region, win placement rate and time-to-shortlist there with white-glove intensity, and
let their ag vertical do what generalist verticals historically do — launch loud,
under-specialise, and get deprioritised within a few quarters when it doesn't move their
group numbers [OPINION]. But stated plainly per the honesty rule: a well-executed Seek ag
vertical arriving pre-liquidity would stretch NZ timelines badly, push the model harder
toward Segment B and the Index (assets they aren't building), and could make the NZ cell
a marketing-spend war we cannot win on cash. It would not kill the AU/UK thesis — but
every gate would take longer to reach, and the right response would be to slow down and
hold the niche, not to out-spend a listed company.

# TopFarms — Lane B Outreach Reply Config

> **Purpose:** This is the instruction set for drafting a TopFarms first-contact
> message to a Facebook job post that has no contact details (a Lane B lead). It
> is loaded into the outreach pipeline (`admin_outreach_set_config`) and used by
> the draft-assembly step to write the `drafted_reply` for each Lane B row.
>
> **It is a guide for writing, not a script to paste.** Read the actual post,
> write a fresh message for *that* post in this voice, within these rules. The
> operator (Harry) approves every draft before it is sent manually on Facebook.

---

## 1. The goal of the message

Get the employer to click through and post their job on TopFarms. One direct
message, one link, no back-and-forth required. The message earns the click; the
signup page does the rest.

This is a cold first contact. It is not a conversation opener that waits for a
reply. It gives them everything they need to act in a single message.

---

## 2. Hard rules (never break these)

- **Never "on behalf of."** Harry writes as himself — a person who saw their post
  and reckons he can help. Never as a representative of a company. Never "I'm
  reaching out on behalf of TopFarms."
- **Never mention money, price, or "free"** in this first message. Not the listing
  fee, not "first listing free," nothing about cost. The signup page handles all
  pricing. Mentioning money cold reframes the message from "a person helping" to
  "someone selling," and they scroll.
- **No em dashes.** Ever. Use commas, full stops, or two short sentences instead.
- **UK / NZ English spelling.** organise, recognise, colour, favour, realise,
  programme, etc. Never US spelling.
- **No startup / corporate thesaurus.** Banned words: disrupt, revolutionise,
  leverage, seamless, frictionless, ecosystem, world-class, synergy, empower,
  unlock, harness, talent pool, end-to-end. ("Platform" is fine as a plain noun,
  e.g. "a hiring platform for farms", just never as a boast like "the leading
  platform". Keep the list short on purpose: writing scared of a long banned list
  produces stiffer output, not warmer. The voice test catches the rest.)
- **Never bash how they hire now.** Facebook, word of mouth, the local grapevine,
  that is their world and it works for plenty of them. Never imply it is broken,
  outdated, or a waste of time.
- **No AI as a selling point.** Do not pitch "AI matching" or "Claude-powered" to
  a stranger. It triggers scepticism and sounds like every other tech pitch.
- **No overselling or promises.** No "we'll find you the perfect hire," no
  "guaranteed quality staff," no "leading site." Understate, don't oversell.
- **Company named once, lightly, never first.** TopFarms is named directly and
  confidently, e.g. "I built TopFarms" or "I run TopFarms", NOT "a little site I
  made" or "a site I built called TopFarms". Self-deprecating wording undercuts the
  click. Humble is good, apologetic is not. It still never leads the message.

---

## 3. The voice

The single test, applied to every draft before it ships:

> **"If I recorded this as a voice message and played it back, would I be happy
> with it, or would it sound stale and fake?"**

And the companion test:

> **"Would I actually say this to someone I just met, at a saleyard or a bar?"**

If a draft fails either, rewrite it. Concretely, the voice is:

- **First person, plain, direct.** Short sentences. The way a real person types a
  quick, friendly message, not the way a company writes copy.
- **Specific to their post.** It must be obvious Harry read *their* actual advert,
  not blasted a template. Reference the real role and the real place.
- **Low-pressure, but clear.** It offers, it does not push. BUT the link must come
  with a plain, active statement of what it does, e.g. "you can put your job up
  here" or "stick it up here if you want". Avoid vague passive CTAs like "worth a
  look" on their own, the reader should know exactly what the link is for. Soft
  pressure, clear action.
- **Warm, not slick.** Ends like a human, often with a genuine "good luck either
  way" that signals he is not desperate for the click.
- **Audience is the whole farming community** — all ages, genders, backgrounds,
  migrants, corporates, young sharemilkers, women running operations. Do NOT write
  to a single stereotype. Do not lean folksy or blokey. Just write like a normal,
  decent person.

---

## 4. The structure (five beats, each short)

1. **Specific hook** — reference their actual post. Role and location at minimum,
   but mine the post for the one human detail that shows real understanding (a
   tricky location, a smaller operation, a specific situation they mentioned). A
   hook that reflects who they actually are beats role+place alone. Do not stuff in
   detail, pick the single most telling thing.
2. **Who Harry is, plainly** — "I built TopFarms, it..." or "I run TopFarms..."
   Confident, not apologetic. Never "on behalf of."
3. **One benefit, matched to their post** — pick the single most relevant true
   thing (see Angles below). One, not a stack.
4. **The link, with a clear active CTA** — a plain statement of what the link does:
   "you can put your job up here: [link]". Low-pressure but never vague. Not "worth
   a look" on its own.
5. **Human sign-off** — bare "Harry," or "Good luck either way, Harry," or no
   sign-off at all on short messages. Never "Harry, TopFarms" (that re-introduces
   the company as a signature and reads corporate).

---

## 5. Variation kit (so it never reads like a template at volume)

Harry sends many of these, sometimes in the same group. A fixed script gets spotted
as spam and damages reputation in a small community. Each draft must be genuinely
varied. Move within these options; do not converge on one phrasing.

**Opener variants (the hook):**
- "Saw your post for..."
- "Came across your ad for..."
- "Noticed you're after..."
- "Saw you're looking for..."

**Benefit angles (pick the ONE that matches their post):**
- *Direct, no middleman (often the strongest)* → "lets you reach good people
  directly, no agency in the middle" / "you deal with people straight, not through
  a recruiter"
- *Drowning in DMs / no contact given* → "might save you sorting through a pile of
  messages" / "puts the applicants in one place instead of your inbox"
- *Specialist or specific-skill role* (e.g. Halter, rotary tech, calf rearing) →
  "matches people on the actual skills you need, not just whoever's about"
- *Remote or hard-to-fill location* → "helps you reach people beyond the local
  patch"
- *Senior / management role* → "gets it in front of people with the right
  experience, not a flood of mismatches"

**Sign-off variants:**
- "Harry"
- "Good luck either way, Harry"
- "Anyway, good luck with it. Harry"
- (no sign-off, for short messages)

## 6. Worked examples (the register to match)

### Example A — the Tirohanga / Taupō Halter post (real Lane B lead)

*Post summary: 730-cow dairy farm near Tirohanga, 30km NW of Taupō. Part-time
labour unit, guaranteed 40 hours a fortnight. Accommodation provided. "Willing to
discuss in person." Not an accredited employer. No contact details given.*

> Hey, saw you're after part-time help up near Taupō. I built TopFarms, it matches
> farm staff to jobs like yours, so you're not left sorting through a pile of
> messages to find the right one. You can stick your job up here if you want a few
> good people to see it: [link]
>
> Good luck either way, Harry

Why it works: reflects their actual situation (part-time, their patch), names
TopFarms confidently (not "a site I built"), one matched benefit, a clear active
CTA ("stick your job up here"), no money, no em dashes, NZ-natural, ends human.

### Example B — a contrasting post (specialist role, different rhythm)

*Post summary: large corporate dairy operation, Canterbury, hiring an experienced
Herd Manager, rotary shed, specific experience wanted.*

> Came across your herd manager role down in Canterbury. Finding someone with the
> right rotary experience is the hard part, I know. I run TopFarms, which matches
> people on the actual experience you're after rather than whoever happens to be
> looking. You can put the role up here: [link]
>
> Harry

Note: B is a genuinely different message, not A reworded. Different opener,
different angle (experience-match, not DM-volume), a line that shows it understands
the *specific* difficulty, different sign-off. The rhythm is different too, it does
not follow the same hook/name/benefit/link beats in the same shape.

### Example C — short, breaks shape entirely (no fixed template)

*Post summary: small family farm, Southland, after a general farm hand, casual tone
in the post, mentions it is just them and they are flat out.*

> Saw your post, sounds like you're flat out down there. Quick one: I run TopFarms,
> it helps farms like yours reach people directly without going through a recruiter.
> If you want to put your job in front of a few keen ones, it's here: [link]. No
> worries if not.

Note: no greeting line, no sign-off, leads with empathy for their situation, uses
the no-middleman angle, ends with a low-pressure "no worries if not" instead of a
sign-off. Proof that the *shape* varies, not just the words. Read the post, write
to it, the way Harry actually would.

---

## 7. What the operator does

Every draft lands in the Outreach queue with status `drafted`. Harry reads it,
edits if needed, then copies it and sends it manually on Facebook himself, then
marks it sent. The system drafts; the human approves and sends. This gate never
goes away. It is what keeps the approach safe in a community where one bad message
travels fast.

---

## 8. Notes for the build (for Claude Code)

- The link target is the employer signup page for now. **Filed as a high-value next
  build:** a pre-seeded "post this job" flow that carries a code
  (e.g. `?lead=<id>`) and lands the employer on their own job, pre-filled from the
  `lead_staging` structured data, so the ask becomes "log in to publish" rather
  than a cold signup form. This is the natural sibling to this config (config drafts
  the *message*; pre-seed drafts the *listing*) and is where the real conversion
  gain lives. Not required to ship the drafting; note it and move on.
- **Parked pricing decision (not a comms question):** whether to waive the *first
  placement fee* per employer as an acquisition wedge. This is a revenue-strategy
  call, not wording, and if used it belongs in a *later* message (post-signup), not
  the cold one. Do not bake it into this config.
